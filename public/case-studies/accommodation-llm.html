Disability Accommodation Advocacy LLM

AI system supporting students in navigating disability accommodation requests

My Role
Led research, study design, and synthesis
My Team
1 researcher, 1 designer, 1 engineer
Timeline
August-December 2025

Summary

Reduced writing burden while revealing critical tensions around trust, over-disclosure, and user control in high-stakes communication.

Context & People

Students seeking disability accommodations must communicate personal, often sensitive needs in formal institutional settings. This process introduces not only logistical complexity, but also emotional and psychological burden.

Students navigate vulnerability, stigma, and uncertainty when drafting requests, often without clear guidance on tone, level of disclosure, or expectations.

As a result, many experience hesitation, overthinking, or avoidance, turning what should be a support system into a barrier to access.

My Contributions

I led the research and evaluation of an LLM-based advocacy assistant designed to support students in drafting accommodation requests.

The system provides a private, low-stakes space for users to externalize their needs, structure communication, and iteratively refine their writing before sharing with institutional stakeholders.

Through scenario-based usability testing, think-aloud protocols, and interviews, I examined how AI support shaped cognitive load, user control, trust, and psychological safety in high-stakes communication.

Key Findings

AI reduces cognitive and emotional burden
Participants described writing as significantly easier, helping them move past hesitation and mental blocks.

Over-automation undermines trust
Users were uncomfortable when the system introduced assumptions or overly personal details.

Control is critical for adoption
Participants preferred AI as a support tool, not a replacement, maintaining authorship over their own narrative.

Social context matters more than correctness
Even well-written outputs failed when tone or audience awareness felt inappropriate.

AI cannot resolve systemic stigma
While the tool reduced effort, users still feared judgment from institutional stakeholders.

Design Implications

Designing AI for sensitive communication requires more than accuracy or efficiency.

  • Prioritize psychological safety over automation
  • Enable user-controlled disclosure
  • Support audience-aware communication
  • Allow adjustable levels of AI assistance
  • Make system behavior and limitations transparent

Impact

This work demonstrates how AI can meaningfully reduce the burden of self-advocacy while also introducing new risks around trust and autonomy.

It contributes design guidance for building responsible AI systems in high-stakes, human-centered contexts.

Reflection & Next Steps

This project strengthened my ability to design and evaluate AI systems in emotionally complex settings.

Moving forward, I would focus on longitudinal deployment in real institutional workflows and expand evaluation across diverse user populations to better understand real-world adoption.
