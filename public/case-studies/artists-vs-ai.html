Artists vs AI: Creator Protection & AI Transparency Research

Research on creator trust, ownership, and platform responsibility in generative AI

My Role
Led end-to-end research, including study design, interviews, synthesis, and model creation
My Team
5 researchers (Team SEEEC)
Timeline
Aug 2025 – Dec 2025

Summary

Identified how lack of transparency and consent drives mistrust, informing platform-level design for ethical AI and creator protection.

Context & People

Small Instagram artists rely on the platform for visibility, identity, and income. The rise of generative AI has disrupted this ecosystem.

Artists feel their work is being used without consent, leading to deep mistrust in both AI systems and platforms.

At the same time, artists hold nuanced, often conflicting views, balancing curiosity about AI with fear of exploitation, replacement, and loss of authorship.

Community norms, peers, and institutions strongly shape how artists express or suppress these views.

My Contributions

I conducted 45-60 minute contextual interviews with 10 small Instagram artists and synthesized 200+ interpretation notes into affinity diagrams and cultural models.

Using directed storytelling, I captured how artists experience AI in real contexts, moments of excitement, fear, conflict, and adaptation.

I translated these insights into system-level design recommendations focused on:

  • Transparency (clear AI labeling, training disclosure)
  • Creator protection (style ownership, anti-scraping mechanisms)
  • Platform accountability (policy + enforcement)
  • Community dynamics (norms, stigma, discourse spaces)

Key Insights

Flip through our online zine for a deeper dive into our research!

If the flipbook does not load here, open it directly at Heyzine.

  • Transparency is the foundation of trust Artists constantly verify whether content is AI-generated, creating cognitive burden and distrust.
  • Artists hold nuanced, but hidden, opinions on AI Many feel forced into polarized pro-AI or anti-AI positions despite more complex beliefs.
  • AI threatens identity, not just labor Beyond economic concerns, artists fear losing authorship, style ownership, and meaning in their work.
  • Self-protection is unsustainable Watermarking and defensive tools are inconsistent and place burden on artists rather than systems.
  • Human creativity is still deeply valued Artists emphasize process, intention, and effort as what gives art meaning, not just output.

Design Directions

  • AI Transparency Infrastructure Automatic labeling of AI-generated or AI-assisted content. Disclosure of training sources and artist inclusion.
  • Creator Protection Systems Style protection, opt-in training, anti-scraping tools.
  • Platform Governance Clear policies for AI usage, attribution, and disclosure.
  • Community Support Mechanisms Spaces for nuanced discussion without stigma. Features that highlight human creative process.

Reflection & Next Steps

This project strengthened my ability to study complex sociotechnical systems where technology, identity, and power intersect.

Next steps include prototyping platform features, such as AI labels and style protection tools, and testing them with artist communities in real-world contexts.
