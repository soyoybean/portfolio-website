FLOWGUARD — Assistive Pain Relief System

Human-centered assistive system for chronic pain relief and autonomy support.

Context & People

Placeholder image for context and people

This project focused on long-term wheelchair users who experience chronic pain and limited autonomy in accessing consistent physical relief. Through conversations and observational insights, it became clear that many assistive tools are designed with automation-first assumptions rather than real user control, leading to discomfort, lack of trust, and low sustained use.

Stakeholders included wheelchair users with varying mobility needs, as well as informal caregivers who often assist in pain management routines. A major pain point was the lack of accessible, user-controllable relief systems that respect autonomy and physical constraints.

My Method & Rationale

Placeholder image for method and rationale

I conducted semi-structured interviews and iterative usability testing to understand user preferences around autonomy, transparency, and physical interaction. Given the embodied and sensitive nature of the use case, qualitative and participatory approaches were prioritized over purely survey-based methods.

I then designed the interaction system, built the hardware prototype (Arduino assembly and coding), and iterated based on user feedback to align the system behavior with real user expectations rather than designer assumptions.

Key Findings & Impact

Placeholder image for findings and impact

A key insight was that users valued predictability and control over automation complexity. This directly led to simplifying the interaction flow and improving feedback mechanisms in the prototype. As a result, later testing sessions showed stronger user comfort and trust in system interactions.

The project resulted in a functional prototype and demonstrated how assistive technologies benefit from transparency-centered interaction design, contributing to applied research in human-centered assistive systems.

Reflection & Next Steps

Placeholder image for reflection and next steps

I learned how to translate sensitive user insights into tangible design iterations, especially in high-stakes contexts involving accessibility and physical wellbeing. Technically, I strengthened my hardware prototyping and iterative usability testing skills.

If I were to redo the project, I would recruit a more diverse range of mobility profiles earlier to capture broader accessibility needs. Next steps would include longitudinal user testing and collaboration with clinicians to evaluate sustained usability in real-world care settings.
